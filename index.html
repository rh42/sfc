01 The Mismatch

Two ways of measuring the same brain tell very different stories.

Structural connectivity is the wiring. Functional connectivity is the pattern of activity riding on top of it. They only partly overlap, and that gap is the whole puzzle. You cannot just lay one map on the other and call it a day.

1.1

The structural connectome is sparse.

These are white-matter pathways estimated from diffusion MRI. Only some region pairs have a direct structural link. That is not a flaw in the map. The brain is just not as well-connected as it thinks it is.

Unimodal regions (V1, V2, A1, S1, M1) sit on the left. Transmodal hubs (PCC, mPFC, dlPFC, IPL, ACC, Ins) sit on the right.

1.2

Functional connectivity is dense — every pair has a relationship.

Now look at resting-state BOLD correlations. Every pair of regions gets a value, even pairs with no wire between them. The pulsing amber pair is one example: structurally unconnected, functionally in sync. The wiring does not explain it. That is the problem.

The mismatch is the whole problem. You cannot just correlate these two matrices — their zero-structures don't agree.

1.3

So how does the signal get there?

Hover any node to see its direct links. Then click the pulsing pair. No direct tract, but the signal finds a way through intermediate regions, like a commute with two changes and a long wait at the second stop. Each hop lights up as the path unfolds.

This indirect path is why we need communication models. They turn a sparse wire-map into a full prediction by modelling how signal walks the graph.

02 How Signals Travel

A sparse wiring diagram
does not predict itself.

To go from anatomy to function, the network needs a theory of how signals get around. Three classic ideas — shortest path, random walk, and communicability — take the same sparse wiring and produce three quite different answers. Think of it as three different theories of how rumours spread through an office.

Same wiring. Now in grid form.

2.2

Same anatomy. Three rules. Three different routes.

Take a pair with no direct wire — PCC → A1 — and ask how each model accounts for their connection. Toggle to see the same question answered three different ways. Same wiring, very different logic.

In one benchmark study, MFPT was the strongest single global predictor of functional connectivity, with communicability close behind it.

2.3

The model does one job: turn A into B.

A is the wiring — sparse, mostly zero. B is the connectivity pattern the model predicts from that wiring. Sparse becomes full. Now there is something to compare with real functional data.

Toggle the model under B. A never changes. B does, because the rule changes. Hover any cell to see its partner on the other side. Two matrices, one comparison. That is SFC. Next: what to do with it.

03 What SFC Measures

The prediction is only useful
if we can score it.

The communication model turned sparse wiring into a dense prediction. Now line that prediction up against what the brain actually does, region by region. The score for how well they match is structure–function coupling. Finally, a number.

3.1

The model made its bet. Here is what the brain did.

Left: what the structural communication model predicted. Right: what the brain actually measured at rest. The two patterns overlap, but not perfectly. The glowing cells mark where the gap is largest.

Hover any cell to see the predicted value, the actual value, and the gap. Small gap: good model. Large gap: the brain had other plans.

3.2

One region. Two rows. The match is the score.

The highlighted row is one region's full connectivity profile across all other regions. Toggle between a sensory region and a default-mode hub. The closer the predicted row tracks the actual row, the higher that region's SFC score.

High match means structure accounts for more of the functional pattern, so SFC is high. Low match means function is less closely aligned with structure, so SFC is low.

3.3

Every region gets a score. A gradient emerges.

Map the SFC score back onto the network. A pattern appears: primary sensory and motor regions tend to score high, while default-mode and other association areas tend to score lower. In other words, structure explains more of the functional pattern in some parts of cortex than in others.

This gradient has been reported across datasets, methods, and age ranges. Lower SFC in association cortex is typically read as flexibility rather than failure: the wiring underdetermines the function, leaving room for a wider range of activity patterns.

· Summary

SFC = how well the wiring predicts the activity pattern.

You have been quietly thinking like a connectome theorist.

Start with sparse anatomy, run it through a communication model, and compare it with what the brain actually does. The match, region by region, is the SFC score: typically higher in sensory systems and lower in association cortex. Once you see SFC, it is hard to unsee it; it becomes a simple way to remember how structure and function fit together.

Across the lifespan
7 → 84

In one large cross-sectional sample spanning ages 7 to 84, SFC was highest in early adulthood and lower in both younger and older participants. Age-related declines were especially marked in sensory and motor regions, even though these regions had the highest SFC in young adults.

Zamani Esfahlani et al., 2022
Heritability
h² = 0.78

Regional SFC shows substantial heritability. In one study, subcortical structure–function coupling reached median h² values around 0.78, comparable to highly heritable traits. Spatial patterns of SFC heritability also differed from SC heritability, pointing to a partly distinct genetic phenotype.

Gu et al., 2021
Individual fingerprint
r = 0.977

In a test–retest subsample scanned twice about six months apart, regional SFC patterns were highly stable: the mean difference across sessions was essentially zero and the test–retest correlation reached r = 0.977. Much of this individual "fingerprint" was carried by transmodal regions, where structure–function alignment is weaker and more person-specific.

Gu et al., 2021
